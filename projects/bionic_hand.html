Bionic Hand

  1. Home
  2. Projects
  3. Bionic Hand

Objective

The aim was to develop a fully functional, authentically moving bionic hand. The costs should remain low and the most basic technology should be used, on the one hand to ensure replicability and on the other hand to be able to present the technology in a way that everyone can understand.

Background

It is now possible to deduce the intended movement using electrical potentials, which are derived from the muscles originally responsible for the movements, and complex algorithms. In the past, electrodes had to be implanted in the muscle tissue for this purpose, but today the deeper evoked potentials can be deduced from the distribution of the charges on the skin (non-invasive). There are probably still several years of development before the use of EEG technology over the motor areas of the cortex to determine movement intention. Since a natural hand not only receives efferent signals, but also picks up sensory information and sends it to the cortex, efforts are being made to integrate this function into a bionic hand. The simplest method is to attach pressure sensors to the bionic hand and apply the measured pressure to intact skin tissue. Over time, the patient will learn to interpret the stimulus experienced as a stimulus from the hand, and gradually the somatosensory neurons of the hand area that were actually intended for the hand and have become unemployed will probably also be included in the processing of these stimuli (reorganization process through neuroplasticity). It is somewhat more complicated, but possible today, to electrically stimulate the afferent nerve tracts of the hand according to the sensor values in such a way that adequate stimuli can be perceived.
The glove and robot hand.

Result

The result is a fully functional robot hand that adequately reproduces the hand movements of the sensor glove wearer. Due to the sensor spacing on the glove, the joint spacing is given and the hand can only be controlled by a person with similar hand proportions to mine. In addition, the wearer's freedom of movement is slightly restricted by the size of the sensors. Since the sensor system is connected to the motor system via cables, the distance between these two components is limited. I wanted to implement the project with the simplest possible means, but had to switch from the pre-programmed RC technology to a microcontroller system to improve the accuracy, but I think that this technology is also easily accessible and understandable for everyone. This example of directly controlled robotics can be used wherever one cannot/must not be at the scene of the action, for example to defuse bombs, handle toxic chemicals or (further on) take on personal protection tasks (IRON MAN).
